As a child, I often heard people say that beauty lies in the eye of the beholder. Whether discussing paintings, music, films, or books, the conclusion was always the same: everyone has different preferences, so no opinion is better than another. For a long time, I accepted this idea without question. It seemed fair and tolerant. Yet the more I learned about art and creativity, the more I realised that personal preference and artistic quality are not exactly the same thing.

Our preferences are undeniably subjective. One person enjoys classical music, while another prefers electronic dance music. Some admire abstract paintings, whereas others are drawn to realistic landscapes. These differences are natural and worth respecting. However, acknowledging personal taste does not necessarily mean that every creative work possesses equal merit.

Imagine two novels. One contains carefully developed characters, a coherent storyline, and thoughtful language. The other is filled with inconsistent plotlines, grammatical mistakes, and little emotional depth. A reader may personally enjoy the second novel more, perhaps because it reminds them of childhood memories or matches their interests. Yet most experienced critics and writers would still recognise the first as a stronger piece of craftsmanship. Personal enjoyment and artistic quality can coexist without always pointing in the same direction.

The same principle applies across many creative fields. A beautifully engineered building demonstrates balance, functionality, and careful planning, even if someone dislikes its architectural style. A skilled musician may perform with exceptional precision despite playing a genre that certain listeners never choose voluntarily. Technical excellence exists independently of individual preference.

Learning to create something oneself makes this distinction even clearer. Anyone who has written a story, composed music, painted a picture, or designed a product quickly discovers that every decision influences the final result. Some choices improve clarity, harmony, and emotional impact, while others weaken them. If quality did not exist, revision would have no purpose. There would be no reason to improve a sentence, adjust a melody, or refine a design because every version would be equally successful.

This does not mean that artistic judgment is completely objective. Unlike mathematics, creative work rarely produces a single correct answer. Instead, quality often emerges through the combination of skill, originality, emotional depth, and effective communication. These characteristics cannot always be measured precisely, but they are recognised consistently enough that experts from different backgrounds frequently admire many of the same works.

Taste itself also develops over time. The more people read, listen, observe, and create, the more they begin to notice subtle qualities that once escaped their attention. An experienced photographer may appreciate composition and lighting that beginners overlook. A trained chef notices flavours and techniques invisible to casual diners. Better taste is not about becoming more fashionable; it is about becoming more perceptive.

At the same time, expertise should not become arrogance. Good taste does not require dismissing popular entertainment or insisting that only certain forms of art deserve appreciation. Instead, it encourages curiosity and a willingness to understand why particular works have endured across generations. Appreciating quality does not prevent anyone from enjoying simpler pleasures.

Ultimately, personal preference and artistic excellence are related but distinct ideas. We are free to like whatever speaks to us personally, yet that freedom does not eliminate the possibility that some works demonstrate greater imagination, craftsmanship, or insight than others. Developing good taste is therefore less about replacing individual preferences and more about learning to recognise excellence wherever it appears.
